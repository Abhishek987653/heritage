Best time to visit Maharashtra

The best time to visit Maharashtra is during the winter months, typically from October to February, when the weather is pleasant and ideal for sightseeing. Temperatures during this period range from 12-25°C, making it comfortable for exploring historical sites, markets, and hill stations.



Natural Parks in Maharashtra

Sanjay Gandhi National Park (Mumbai)

Located in the heart of Mumbai, Sanjay Gandhi National Park is a unique forest reserve spread over 103 sq. km. It is known for its rich biodiversity and the ancient Kanheri Caves, a major historical attraction within the park. Despite being surrounded by urban sprawl, it supports leopards, deer, langurs, and over 270 bird species. The park also offers activities like boating, nature trails, and a toy train ride. It's a vital green lung for the city and attracts millions annually.

Tadoba Andhari Tiger Reserve (Chandrapur)

Tadoba is Maharashtra’s oldest and largest national park, covering 1,727 sq. km. It’s renowned for tiger sightings, along with leopards, sloth bears, and gaur. The reserve features teak forests, bamboo groves, and lakes, including the picturesque Tadoba Lake. Safari tours are popular here, drawing wildlife enthusiasts and photographers. Tadoba stands out for its thriving tiger population and commitment to conservation-based tourism.

Navegaon National Park (Gondia)

Spread over 135 sq. km, Navegaon is famous for its serene lake and rich birdlife, with over 200 species including migratory birds. It also shelters leopards, bears, and hyenas. The park’s centerpiece is the Navegaon Lake, ideal for boating and birdwatching. The Dr. Salim Ali Bird Interpretation Center adds educational value. With its mix of eco-tourism and wildlife, it’s a peaceful retreat for nature lovers.

Chandoli National Park (Sangli, Kolhapur, Satara)

Chandoli, covering around 317 sq. km, lies in the Western Ghats and forms part of the Sahyadri Tiger Reserve. The park is characterized by rugged hills, waterfalls, and dense forests. Wildlife includes tigers, leopards, Indian bison, and sloth bears. The park also contains historical remnants like the Prachitgad Fort. Chandoli is lesser-known but ecologically important, playing a vital role in the corridor for tiger movement.

Melghat Tiger Reserve (Amravati)

Located in the Satpura hill range, Melghat was one of India’s first nine tiger reserves. Spanning over 1,500 sq. km, it is a haven for Bengal tigers, leopards, and a variety of birds and reptiles. The rugged terrain includes deep valleys and high cliffs, with the Tapti River cutting through. Melghat is rich in biodiversity and also houses tribal communities who coexist with the forest ecosystem.

Pench National Park (Nagpur district – Maharashtra side)

Pench, shared with Madhya Pradesh, is famous for its rich wildlife and as the inspiration for Rudyard Kipling’s The Jungle Book. The Maharashtra side spans around 257 sq. km and is home to tigers, leopards, wild dogs, and many herbivores. The park features teak forests and open grasslands, making it ideal for jeep safaris. It supports both conservation and eco-tourism in the Vidarbha region.
